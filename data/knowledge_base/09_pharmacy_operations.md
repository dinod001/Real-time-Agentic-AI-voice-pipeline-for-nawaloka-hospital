# Nawaloka Hospital — Pharmacy Operations Manual

## 1. Department Overview

The Nawaloka Pharmacy Department comprises the **Main Pharmacy** (Ground Floor), **Inpatient Pharmacy** (1st Floor), **Oncology Pharmacy** (Cytotoxic Reconstitution Unit, 4th Floor), and **Emergency Pharmacy** (ED, Ground Floor). The department maintains a formulary of approximately 1,800 medications.

### 1.1 Operating Hours

| Unit | Hours | Staffing |
|------|-------|----------|
| Main (Outpatient) Pharmacy | 07:00–22:00 daily | 3 pharmacists + 4 dispensers per shift |
| Inpatient Pharmacy | 24/7 | 2 pharmacists + 2 dispensers (night shift: 1+1) |
| ED Pharmacy | 24/7 | 1 pharmacist + 1 dispenser |
| Oncology Pharmacy | 08:00–18:00 Mon–Sat | 1 oncology pharmacist + 1 trained technician |

### 1.2 Formulary Management

The Drug & Therapeutics Committee (DTC) meets quarterly to review the hospital formulary. New drug requests must be submitted on Form PH-01 with supporting evidence (RCTs, guidelines, cost-effectiveness data). Non-formulary medications require DTC chair approval and are procured on a named-patient basis.

## 2. Prescription Processing

### 2.1 Inpatient Prescriptions

1. The physician enters the prescription electronically via the HIS (CPOE — Computerised Physician Order Entry) or writes a legible paper prescription during system downtime.
2. The pharmacist reviews the order for: correct dose, route, frequency, duration, drug interactions, duplicate therapy, renal/hepatic dose adjustments, and allergy contraindications.
3. Clinical interventions (dose changes, substitutions) are documented in the Pharmacist Intervention Log and communicated to the prescriber.
4. Medications are dispensed in unit-dose packaging where available. Ward stock items are replenished via the automated dispensing cabinets (Omnicell XT) located in each nursing station.

### 2.2 Outpatient Prescriptions

1. The patient presents the consultant's prescription at the Main Pharmacy counter.
2. The pharmacist verifies the prescription, dispenses medications, and provides counselling on: dose, frequency, storage, common side effects, and when to seek medical attention.
3. Average dispensing time target: ≤ 15 minutes from presentation.
4. Controlled substances require a separate prescription form (Form PH-CS-01) — see Section 5.

### 2.3 Discharge Prescriptions

1. Discharge medications are prepared by the Inpatient Pharmacy within 2 hours of receiving the discharge order.
2. The pharmacist conducts a final medication reconciliation against the admission medication list.
3. Patient counselling at discharge covers: new medications, changed medications, discontinued medications, follow-up instructions.

## 3. Drug Storage & Inventory

### 3.1 Storage Conditions

| Condition | Temperature | Examples |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Room temperature | 15–25°C | Most oral medications, topicals |
| Refrigerated | 2–8°C | Insulins, vaccines, some antibiotics (amoxicillin suspension), biologics |
| Frozen | -20°C or below | Certain biologics, some sera |
| Light-protected | Amber containers or foil wrapping | Methotrexate, nifedipine, sodium nitroprusside |

Temperature is continuously monitored using digital data loggers. Excursions outside the specified range trigger an alert to the on-duty pharmacist who assesses the affected stock for usability.

### 3.2 Inventory Management

- **ABC Analysis**: Class A drugs (top 20% by value — ~80% of spend) are reviewed weekly. Class B (next 30%) monthly. Class C (remaining 50%) quarterly.
- **PAR (Periodic Automatic Replenishment)**: Ward stock levels are defined per item; nursing staff trigger reorders when stock falls to the reorder point.
- **Expiry Monitoring**: The FEFO (First Expiry, First Out) principle is enforced. Items within 3 months of expiry are flagged in the inventory system for use-up or return to the supplier.
- **Emergency Stock**: A 7-day emergency buffer of critical medications (adrenaline, atropine, naloxone, insulin, broad-spectrum antibiotics, blood products anticoagulants) is maintained.

## 4. Drug Interactions & Clinical Pharmacy

### 4.1 Drug Interaction Checking

The CPOE system integrates a drug interaction database (Lexicomp / Micromedex). Alerts are classified as:

| Severity | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Contraindicated (X) | Order cannot proceed; pharmacist contacts prescriber |
| Major (D) | Alert displayed; pharmacist reviews and intervenes if needed |
| Moderate (C) | Alert displayed; monitoring recommended |
| Minor (B) | Information only |

### 4.2 Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM)

Medications requiring TDM at Nawaloka:

| Drug | Target Trough Level | Sampling Time |
|------|---------------------|---------------|
| Vancomycin | 15–20 mg/L (AUC/MIC-guided preferred) | 30 min before 4th dose |
| Gentamicin (conventional) | < 2 mg/L (trough) | 30 min before next dose |
| Phenytoin | 10–20 mg/L (total); 1–2 mg/L (free) | At steady state (~7 days) |
| Carbamazepine | 4–12 mg/L | At steady state (~2 weeks) |
| Lithium | 0.6–1.0 mmol/L | 12 hours post-dose |
| Digoxin | 0.8–2.0 ng/mL | ≥ 6 hours post-dose |
| Cyclosporine | 100–300 ng/mL (varies by indication) | Trough (C₀) |
| Tacrolimus | 5–15 ng/mL (varies by indication) | Trough (C₀) |

The clinical pharmacist reviews TDM results and provides dose adjustment recommendations to the treating team.

### 4.3 Antimicrobial Stewardship (Pharmacy Role)

The ASP Pharmacist is a key member of the Antimicrobial Stewardship Programme:
- Reviews all restricted antibiotic prescriptions within 24 hours.
- Provides IV-to-oral switch recommendations at 48–72 hours.
- Performs prospective audit and feedback of antibiotic prescribing.
- Maintains and distributes the annual Nawaloka Antibiogram (organism-specific susceptibility data).

## 5. Controlled Substances Management

### 5.1 Schedule & Classification

Nawaloka follows the National Dangerous Drugs Control Board classifications:

| Schedule | Examples | Storage |
|----------|----------|---------|
| Schedule I | Morphine, fentanyl, pethidine, methadone | Double-locked safe; two-person access |
| Schedule II | Codeine (> 30 mg), diazepam, midazolam | Locked cupboard; single-key access |
| Schedule III | Codeine (≤ 30 mg), tramadol (some formulations) | Separate shelf within the pharmacy |

### 5.2 Dispensing Controls

1. Prescriptions for Schedule I drugs must be written on the dedicated triplicate form (PH-CS-01) and signed by a consultant or senior MO.
2. The pharmacist verifies the prescription, counts the exact quantity, and both the pharmacist and dispenser sign the Controlled Drug Register.
3. Ward stock of controlled drugs is counted at every shift change by two registered nurses; discrepancies are reported immediately to the Pharmacy Manager and the Compliance Officer.
4. Partially used ampoules of Schedule I drugs (e.g., morphine drawn but not fully administered) must be wasted in the presence of a witness; both sign the waste log.

### 5.3 Audit

A full physical audit of all controlled substances is conducted monthly by the Chief Pharmacist and quarterly by an external auditor from the Ministry of Health.

## 6. Cytotoxic Drug Handling

### 6.1 Reconstitution

All cytotoxic drugs are reconstituted in the dedicated **Cytotoxic Pharmacy Clean Room** (ISO Class 5, negative pressure relative to the corridor, with a biological safety cabinet — BSC Class II Type B2).

Only trained oncology pharmacists and technicians prepare cytotoxic drugs. They wear: double gloves (inner non-latex, outer chemotherapy-rated), disposable gown, N95 respirator, and safety goggles.

### 6.2 Transport

Reconstituted cytotoxic drugs are transported to the ward in sealed, labelled, spill-proof containers with "Cytotoxic Hazard" labels. A dedicated cytotoxic spill kit accompanies every delivery.

### 6.3 Spill Management

In the event of a cytotoxic spill:
1. Evacuate non-essential personnel from the area.
2. Don the cytotoxic spill kit PPE.
3. Contain the spill with absorbent pads.
4. Clean from the outside in; double-bag all contaminated materials into cytotoxic waste bags (purple).
5. Report via the Safety Reporting System.

## 7. Adverse Drug Reaction (ADR) Reporting

All suspected ADRs must be reported:
- **Internally**: Via the SRS (Safety Reporting System) within 24 hours.
- **Externally**: Serious and unexpected ADRs are reported to the National Medicines Regulatory Authority (NMRA) using the Yellow Card system within 72 hours.

The Pharmacovigilance Committee reviews ADR trends quarterly and updates the hospital formulary notes accordingly.
