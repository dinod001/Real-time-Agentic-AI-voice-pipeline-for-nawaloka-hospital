# Nawaloka Hospital — Clinical Protocols & Standard Operating Procedures

## 1. Patient Admission Protocol

### 1.1 Planned Admissions

1. Patient presents the consultant's admission order and NIC/passport at the Admissions Counter (Ground Floor, Counter 3–5).
2. The admissions clerk verifies insurance / corporate guarantee and collects the advance deposit (minimum LKR 25,000 for general ward; LKR 75,000 for private rooms).
3. A unique Hospital Number (HN) is generated in the HIS (Hospital Information System) if the patient is new.
4. The clerk assigns a bed based on the consultant's ward preference and current occupancy.
5. The patient receives an admission pack containing: wrist ID band, patient rights leaflet, meal-preference card, and ward orientation guide.
6. The ward nurse performs the initial nursing assessment within 30 minutes of arrival (vital signs, allergy check, fall-risk screening using the Morse Fall Scale).

### 1.2 Emergency Admissions

1. The patient is triaged in the Emergency Department (ED) using the 5-level Nawaloka Triage System (see Section 2).
2. The ED physician stabilises the patient and contacts the on-call consultant.
3. Bed allocation is managed by the Bed Management Unit (ext. 1200), which maintains a live dashboard of available beds.
4. If no bed is available within 4 hours, the Duty Manager must be notified; patients may be held in the ED Observation Unit (max 12 hours).

### 1.3 Admission Documentation Checklist

- Admission order signed by the consultant.
- Consent for treatment (Form ADM-01).
- Allergy wrist band (red for known allergies, green for NKDA — No Known Drug Allergies).
- Medication reconciliation completed (current medications listed in the MAR — Medication Administration Record).
- VTE (Venous Thromboembolism) risk assessment using the Caprini Score.

## 2. Triage System

Nawaloka uses a 5-level triage system adapted from the Canadian Triage and Acuity Scale (CTAS):

| Level | Category | Response Time | Examples |
|-------|----------|---------------|----------|
| 1 | Resuscitation | Immediate | Cardiac arrest, severe trauma, anaphylaxis |
| 2 | Emergent | ≤ 15 min | Chest pain, stroke symptoms, active haemorrhage |
| 3 | Urgent | ≤ 30 min | Abdominal pain, high fever (>39°C), fractures |
| 4 | Less Urgent | ≤ 60 min | Minor lacerations, mild asthma, UTI symptoms |
| 5 | Non-Urgent | ≤ 120 min | Prescription refills, minor rashes, chronic complaints |

Triage is performed by a registered nurse with at least 3 years of ED experience who has completed the Nawaloka Triage Certification course.

## 3. Patient Handover Protocol (ISBAR)

All clinical handovers must use the **ISBAR** framework:

- **I — Identify**: State your name, role, and the patient's name + HN.
- **S — Situation**: Briefly describe the current clinical situation.
- **B — Background**: Relevant history, diagnosis, and treatment so far.
- **A — Assessment**: Your clinical assessment and current status.
- **R — Recommendation**: What you need from the receiving clinician.

Handovers occur at every shift change (06:00, 14:00, 22:00) at the nursing station. The outgoing nurse documents the handover in the electronic nursing notes before leaving.

## 4. Discharge Protocol

### 4.1 Planned Discharge

1. The consultant writes the discharge order in the HIS at least 12 hours in advance when possible.
2. The ward pharmacist reviews the discharge prescriptions and prepares take-home medications.
3. The discharge nurse completes the Discharge Checklist:
   - Patient education on medications, wound care, diet, and follow-up.
   - Follow-up appointment booked (within 7–14 days unless specified).
   - Discharge summary printed and handed to the patient (copy retained in the medical record).
   - Transport arranged if needed (hospital shuttle or ambulance transfer).
4. The billing department prepares the final bill. Insurance patients receive a pre-authorisation confirmation; cash patients settle at Counter 8.
5. The patient returns the wrist ID band and signs the discharge acknowledgement form (Form DCH-02).

### 4.2 Against Medical Advice (AMA) Discharge

If a patient insists on leaving before the consultant recommends discharge:
1. The attending physician documents a discussion of risks in the medical record.
2. The patient signs the AMA Discharge Form (Form AMA-01).
3. The ward nurse informs the consultant by phone.
4. A follow-up call is made by the Patient Relations team within 24 hours.

## 5. Medication Administration — Five Rights

All clinical staff must verify the **Five Rights** before administering any medication:

1. **Right Patient** — Check the wrist band (name + HN).
2. **Right Medication** — Cross-check with the MAR.
3. **Right Dose** — Verify units, concentration, and volume.
4. **Right Route** — Oral, IV, IM, SC, etc.
5. **Right Time** — Within 30 minutes of the scheduled time.

High-alert medications (insulin, anticoagulants, concentrated electrolytes, chemotherapy agents) require independent double-checking by a second registered nurse before administration.

## 6. Blood Transfusion Protocol

1. Blood request form (Form BT-01) must be completed by the treating physician with two patient identifiers.
2. Blood bank cross-matches and issues the unit; each unit is labelled with the patient's details and an expiry time (4 hours from issue).
3. Two registered nurses verify the patient identity, blood group, unit number, and expiry at the bedside.
4. Transfusion is started at a slow rate (2 mL/min) for the first 15 minutes; vital signs are recorded at 0, 15, 30, 60 min and then hourly.
5. Any suspected transfusion reaction → Stop immediately, maintain IV access with normal saline, and notify the blood bank.

## 7. Surgical Safety Checklist (WHO-Based)

Nawaloka mandates the 3-phase WHO Surgical Safety Checklist for every operative procedure:

### Phase 1 — Sign In (Before Anaesthesia)
- Patient identity confirmed.
- Surgical site marked (with indelible marker by the surgeon).
- Consent form signed and in the file.
- Pulse oximeter on the patient and functioning.
- Known allergies confirmed.
- Anaesthesia safety check completed.

### Phase 2 — Time Out (Before Skin Incision)
- All team members introduce themselves by name and role.
- Surgeon confirms: patient name, procedure, and incision site.
- Anticipated critical events discussed (blood loss, special equipment).
- Antibiotic prophylaxis given within the last 60 minutes.
- Essential imaging displayed.

### Phase 3 — Sign Out (Before Patient Leaves OR)
- Procedure name recorded.
- Instrument, sponge, and needle counts completed and correct.
- Specimens labelled.
- Equipment problems documented.
- Key concerns for recovery communicated.
